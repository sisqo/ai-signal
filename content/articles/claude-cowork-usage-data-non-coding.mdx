This week Anthropic gave [Claude Cowork](https://claude.com/product/cowork) — its agentic AI tool for office work, not code — a phone number, a browser tab, and the ability to keep working after a laptop closes. The more interesting number came from the same announcement, not the feature list: of 1.2 million anonymized Cowork sessions Anthropic sampled between May 11 and May 31 across more than 600,000 organizations, only 8.7% were software development, according to [VentureBeat](https://venturebeat.com/technology/anthropic-brings-claude-cowork-to-mobile-and-web-as-usage-data-shows-most-users-arent-coding). Anthropic makes most of its name on coding models. The tool it just expanded onto every device in someone's pocket is used, by its own data, for almost anything but that.

Cowork is what's called an agentic tool: instead of just answering a question in a chat window, it can open files, fill in spreadsheets, draft and send email, and work through a multi-step task on its own, checking back in only when it needs a decision or a permission. [Claude Code](/articles/claude-code-built-in-commands-ranked), Anthropic's tool for that same kind of autonomous, multi-step work applied to software, is the product most coverage of Anthropic focuses on. Cowork is the same idea, aimed at everyone else in the building.

## What actually changed this week

Starting July 7, Cowork sessions move off a single laptop entirely. A task started at a desk keeps running in the cloud after the laptop closes, sends status updates to a phone, and can be picked up from a browser later — Anthropic pitched the update around exactly that gap: "work like that doesn't fit in one sitting... it accumulates overnight, between meetings, on the train," the company said, [according to PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/news/artificial-intelligence/2026/anthropic-launches-mobile-access-for-claude-cowork/). The same release adds Microsoft 365 write access, so Cowork can draft and send email or update OneDrive and SharePoint files, not just read them. The mobile and web rollout starts with Max subscribers only; Anthropic says other plans get access in the coming weeks, even though Cowork's desktop app has already been included on Pro, Team, and Enterprise plans since it launched in January.

## The number Anthropic didn't have to publish

Shipping a mobile app doesn't require publishing a usage breakdown. That Anthropic did anyway is the more telling decision. Business process work — reports, reconciliation, checklists — accounted for 33.4% of sessions, the largest single category. Content creation and copywriting, drafts, presentations, and proposals, came in at 16.4%. Software development trailed both at 8.7%. Anthropic's own framing, [per TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/07/the-coding-agent-wars-are-spilling-into-the-rest-of-the-office-claude-cowork/): "While coding is still — understandably — one of the uses of AI that gets the most attention, the use of AI for everyday business work is on the rise."

## Why a coding company keeps building tools nobody codes with

This isn't a surprise reversal. Cowork launched in January 2026 explicitly built for the average office worker, not the average developer. What's new is that six months of real session data confirms the positioning actually worked as intended, rather than the desktop app quietly turning into one more coding tool that marketing described as broader than it was. The same pattern is showing up industry-wide: OpenAI has been pushing Codex, its own coding-agent brand, into non-developer tasks like report generation and spreadsheet analysis, per the same TechCrunch report. Compare that to [Augment Code's Intent](/articles/augment-code-intent-agent-orchestration), which went the opposite direction this month — a workspace built specifically to coordinate multiple coding agents for developers who already have too many of them running at once. Cowork and Intent are both agentic tools solving a coordination problem; they've just concluded that the person drowning in coordination work is at least as likely to work in marketing or legal as in an IDE.

## What this means if you're deciding whether to use it

If your team is evaluating Cowork, the roughly 91% of usage that isn't coding is a more useful reference point than anything in Anthropic's coding benchmarks — the tool is being validated, in practice, on reconciling a spreadsheet or building a slide deck, not on writing a pull request. Rolling it out to non-technical staff is also where the fine print matters most: Cowork's enterprise controls let admins set spending limits and tool permissions by department precisely because the same product that reads a shared drive can also write to it, draft an email in someone's name, or touch a CRM record without a developer in the loop to notice something odd. The mobile and web rollout is Max-only for now — if your organization runs on Pro or standard Team seats, budget for a wait rather than assuming this week's announcement applies to your plan immediately.
